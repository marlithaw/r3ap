The Studio Toolkit

De-Escalation Stages

"Because a dysregulated adult cannot help regulate a child, teachers need automatic, predictable responses."

Master the 5 stages of a behavioral crisis so you never have to improvise when the volume goes up.

1

The Trigger Phase

Students send subtle warning signals that they are becoming overwhelmed, such as sighing, clenching fists, or lingering.

When you see: A student showing early signs of frustration or refusing to transition...
The Studio Response: Intervene early and low-key. Acknowledge the challenge and offer a brief regulation break (e.g., "I can see it's hard to switch gears. Take a minute to get a drink of water, then we'll get started.")
When you see: Minor, low-level disruptions (tapping, whispering)...
The Studio Response: Use "strategic ignoring" for behaviors that don't impact safety, or use proximity (standing near the student) to redirect them without a verbal reprimand.
2

The Agitation Phase

If the trigger isn't addressed, the student may push back, argue slightly, or slam materials.

When you see: A student begins to argue or challenge your authority...
The Studio Response: Drop your volume, slow your pace, and completely avoid power struggles.
When you see: The student expressing anger or defiance...
The Studio Response: Validate feelings without validating the behavior. Use empathy ("I see you're frustrated") and offer 2-3 acceptable choices ("You can complete this independently or with a partner. Which do you prefer?").
3

The Acceleration Phase

The student becomes louder, paces, or blatantly refuses. Reasoning is no longer effective.

When you see: The student is highly agitated and raising their voice...
The Studio Response: Use significantly fewer words and maintain a calm, monotonous tone. Do not ask "Why?" questions or deliver lectures.
When you see: Verbal directions are causing more pushback...
The Studio Response: Lean on non-verbal cues. Quietly point to a "Take a Break" card or gesture toward a calm space to reduce cognitive demand.
4

The Crisis Phase

Peak escalation. Unsafe behavior or extreme disruption. Safety is the only priority.

When you see: The student becomes unsafe or highly disruptive...
The Studio Response: Stop teaching. Follow district safety protocols (call an admin, give space, or escort others out).
When you see: You must interact during the crisis...
The Studio Response: Use the absolute minimum number of words possible (e.g., "I am here when you are ready").
When you feel: Your own heart racing and anxiety rising...
The Studio Response: Step back and take 3 deep breaths. Your panic will only escalate their aggression.
5

The Recovery Phase

The student is settling, but remains highly vulnerable to being re-triggered.

When you see: The student begins to calm down...
The Studio Response: Acknowledge their effort gently, without rushing into discipline ("I'm glad you're feeling calmer. Take a few more minutes.").
When you feel: It's time to process behavior or assign consequences...
The Studio Response: Wait at least 20–30 minutes until fully baseline. Logic too soon restarts the crisis cycle.
Physical Stance

The Studio Posture

Body language communicates safety or threat to a dysregulated brain. Memorize these automatic physical responses.

When approaching:

Stay at least 4x your usual distance (~6 feet) away.

When speaking:

Stand at an angle (the "L-stance") rather than face-to-face.

When they sit:

Get on their eye level by sitting or crouching.

When directing:

Keep hands visible. Never point or shake your finger at them.
